Encoding Principles

"Affliction [1]" is part of a comprehensive edition of George Herbert's English verse, The Digital Temple. This larger project includes computer-readable transcriptions, in both original- and modern-spelling versions, of Williams MS. Jones B62, Bodleian MS. Tanner 307, and a British Library copy of The Temple: Sacred Poems and Private Ejaculations, first edition (Cambridge, 1633); high-resolution digital images of the sources (excepting the Latin verse in Williams); an apparatus that includes critical, textual, and technical introductions and annotations; and a user interface with which to navigate these materials.

Transcriptions are encoded in TEI(P5)-conformant XML.

Currently, only the Williams images and transcriptions are captured directly from the source. Images and transcriptions of the Bodleian MS. and first edition are captured from Scolar black-and-white facsimiles.

Apparent errors are preserved and editorial corrections provided using SIC and CORR tags, but only where the editor conjectures that the original scribe or compositor would have recognized the instance as an error. For example, what according to modern usage is incorrect subject/verb agreement might have been deemed acceptable to a seventeenth-century scribe or compositor. All such instances are treated instead as normalizations. (See below.)

Original spellings, abbreviations, and orthography are preserved and normalizations provided. Where in the manuscripts a character's status as majuscule or miniscule is ambiguous, the editor has silently chosen one or the other based on context and judgment (i.e., does not register such ambiguity in the notes).

Original quotation marks, if any, are preserved.

Original hyphenation is preserved.

Critical note: Lines 65-66: "Even though it would mean ceasing to be in any meaningful sense, I would rather you not prod me to love you at all if, as it seems, I fail continuously to do so—or do so poorly." (A pun on the second not, i.e., naught, is supported by its alternative spelling in #w, nott.) The rapid volte face in lines 61-64 captures in miniature the poem's larger movement; it is in these final lines, however, that the speaker steps back and succinctly analyzes his dilemma even while maintaining a rhetorical stance that challenges his interlocutor. The repetition and reversal in the final line (what Wilcox identifies as antimetabole) might also be a moment, rare in Herbert, of predestinarian exasperation in which is implied a rhetorical question (erotema): "Why bother puttting me through this if I am not in the end one of the fortunate who really love you (as opposed to those who only think they do)?" One possible answer, a projection of the speaker's "simpering" side, is that there is no good reason for his pains and that the fate of the reprobate is an impenetrable mystery. The more favorable (and no less mysterious) answer is that the speaker's love for God is entirely inadequate yet made sufficient through grace.

Critical note: Lines 65-66: "Even though it would mean ceasing to be in any meaningful sense, I would rather you not prod me to love you at all if, as it seems, I fail continuously to do so—or do so poorly." (A pun on the second not, i.e., naught, is supported by its alternative spelling in #w, nott.) The rapid volte face in lines 61-64 captures in miniature the poem's larger movement; it is in these final lines, however, that the speaker steps back and succinctly analyzes his dilemma even while maintaining a rhetorical stance that challenges his interlocutor. The repetition and reversal in the final line (what Wilcox identifies as antimetabole) might also be a moment, rare in Herbert, of predestinarian exasperation in which is implied a rhetorical question (erotema): "Why bother puttting me through this if I am not in the end one of the fortunate who really love you (as opposed to those who only think they do)?" One possible answer, a projection of the speaker's "simpering" side, is that there is no good reason for his pains and that the fate of the reprobate is an impenetrable mystery. The more favorable (and no less mysterious) answer is that the speaker's love for God is entirely inadequate yet made sufficient through grace.

Line number 66
Critical note: Lines 65-66: "Even though it would mean ceasing to be in any meaningful sense, I would rather you not prod me to love you at all if, as it seems, I fail continuously to do so—or do so poorly." (A pun on the second not, i.e., naught, is supported by its alternative spelling in #w, nott.) The rapid volte face in lines 61-64 captures in miniature the poem's larger movement; it is in these final lines, however, that the speaker steps back and succinctly analyzes his dilemma even while maintaining a rhetorical stance that challenges his interlocutor. The repetition and reversal in the final line (what Wilcox identifies as antimetabole) might also be a moment, rare in Herbert, of predestinarian exasperation in which is implied a rhetorical question (erotema): "Why bother puttting me through this if I am not in the end one of the fortunate who really love you (as opposed to those who only think they do)?" One possible answer, a projection of the speaker's "simpering" side, is that there is no good reason for his pains and that the fate of the reprobate is an impenetrable mystery. The more favorable (and no less mysterious) answer is that the speaker's love for God is entirely inadequate yet made sufficient through grace.
